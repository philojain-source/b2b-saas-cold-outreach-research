## 1. ~2026-07-01 — "Cold email is not a passive game" (call on every signal)

> One of our clients gets the lowest positive-reply rate across all our clients, yet
> they're top-tier in meetings booked per week. Here's how:
> - Anytime someone accepts a LinkedIn connection request → they call them.
> - Anytime someone responds to a LinkedIn message (positive, negative, or neutral) → call.
> - Anytime someone responds to an email (unless it's a straight "not interested") → call.
> - Anytime they're referred to someone → they call ASAP.
> And then they keep calling. Then follow up on LinkedIn and email, multiple times. Then
> they call other people in the organization. If you want to squeeze the most juice out of
> cold email, this is the way.

*Why it matters:* the highest-output play here isn't better copy — it's phone follow-up on
every micro-signal instead of waiting for inbound replies.

## 2. ~2026-06-24 — Add a secondary CTA to lift reply rates

> Adding a secondary CTA has dramatically improved reply rates across a lot of our
> campaigns. The first CTA is the main ask (book a call / reply to learn more). The second
> is a softer option for people who aren't quite there yet:
> - "Or if you'd rather I just send a short video of exactly why I reached out and how I think we can help, let me know."
> - "Happy to send a couple examples of companies like yours we've worked with."
> - "Or would it make more sense to connect with [Colleague]?"
> - "I'm sure you have a lot of competing priorities — mind if I check back in a few months?"
> We put these right after the first CTA, or in the P.S. line.

*Why it matters:* a low-friction second option converts the "interested but not ready"
segment that a single hard CTA loses.

## 3. ~2026-06-24 — "Founder tone" for small companies

> I've worked with a lot of small clients competing against much bigger players. Our
> campaigns always flopped when I tried to make them sound like a big company. Now I lean
> into "founder tone" — the email feels like the founder found that specific company and
> sent off a quick email. Short, casual, direct, and often with a typo or two. Everyone
> knows smaller companies offer a more personal experience, so lean into it.

*Why it matters:* match the copy to the sender's actual advantage — being small and
personal beats imitating enterprise polish.
