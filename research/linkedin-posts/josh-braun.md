## 1. ~2026-07-08 — Cold-call closing: why the "illusion of control" backfires

> I heard a salesperson end a cold call with: "Would Wednesday at 10 work? Or Thursday at 2? Or Friday at 3?"
>
> They were probably taught a technique called the illusion of control. The idea is simple: instead of asking whether someone wants a meeting, you assume they do and let them choose the time. It sounds clever.
>
> But to me, it feels like the decision has already been made for me. Ironically, when someone does this, I'm more likely to pick a time… and then not show up. Not because I'm trying to be rude. Because I don't like feeling boxed in. And I don't want to say no because I know the seller will try to handle the objection.
>
> I'd rather hear: "Would you like to see how this works or not really?" That gives me something many sales techniques accidentally take away: Autonomy.
>
> The golden rule? Sell unto others as you'd want them to sell to you.

*Why it matters:* permission-based closing on cold calls; giving the prospect an easy "no"
increases genuine yeses.

## 2. ~2026-07-08 — Objections are information, not something to "handle"

> I never liked objection handling training. Why? It assumes the buyer is wrong. And the seller's job is to overcome it. The problem? People don't like being told what to do or how to think — especially by a seller who has a vested interest. Whenever people feel like a seller is trying to change their mind, they enter the Zone of Resistance.
>
> So in my workshops, I teach information understanding. When someone says "It's too expensive," "I need to think about it," "We're already using someone else" — I don't hear objections. I hear information. Information about how they're thinking, what's missing, what they value, what they're worried about.
>
> "It's too expensive." → "Sounds like there's a ceiling to what you want to invest."
> "I need to think about it." → "You're skeptical a tool can solve your commission problems."
> "We're already using someone else." → "That's okay. I'm guessing what you're using is working perfectly."
>
> Once you understand someone's thinking, the conversation changes. You stop trying to handle objections. You start understanding people.

*Why it matters:* reframes objection handling for cold conversations — label the thinking
instead of overcoming it.

## 3. ~2026-07-08 — Describe the prospect's reality (personalization that builds trust)

> What do you notice about the difference between how Matt and Mandy respond? [To someone who says "I'm tired":] Matt: "You should take a nap." "Go to bed earlier." Mandy: "Your body feels like concrete." "Everyone sees you smiling, working, standing… and assumes you're full of energy."
>
> Matt hears the words. Mandy hears the experience. Matt responds to the problem. Mandy responds to the person. That's why Mandy makes people feel understood… The fastest way to build trust isn't having the best advice. It's describing someone else's reality. Don't race to fix. Linger in the understanding.

*Why it matters:* the "describe their reality" principle behind his cold-email
personalization (show you understand the prospect's world before pitching).
